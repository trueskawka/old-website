As a good starting point for ideas to hopefully come, here are the five elements of effective thinking (which sound a lot like steps for learning anything):

  • understand deeply

    Don’t face complex issues head-on; first understand simple ideas deeply. Be brutally honest about what you know and don’t know. Then see what’s missing, identify the gaps, and fill them in. There are degrees to understanding and you can always heighten yours.

  • make mistakes

    Fail to succeed. Intentionally get it wrong to inevitably get it even more right. Mistakes highlight unforeseen opportunities and holes in your understanding.

  • raise questions

    Constantly create questions to clarify and extend your understanding.

  • follow ideas

    Look back to see where ideas came from and then look ahead to discover where those ideas may lead. Following the consequences of small ideas can result in big payoffs.

  • change

    You can always improve, grow, and extract more out of your education. Change is the universal constant that allows you to get the most out of learning.

    • ask the person to repeat their comment, to avoid ambiguity (e.g. what did you say? what do you mean by that? excuse me?)
    • label the comment/language (e.g. that’s ignorant/sexist/dismissive), not the person
    • get back to work (after all, you are paid to work and hopefully so is the other person)

  • unwelcome behaviour

    Instead of reacting on the spot, it’s best to talk in person, e.g. “I feel like you interrupt me, have you noticed?” - that’s a simple yes or no question and both answers are great!

    1. yes - “good! please don’t do that again”
    2. no - “no problem, how about I just let you know if I notice it again?” (opening for further improvement)

It is much easier to react and solve problems when we can name them. And we don’t feel crazy, because it’s not “just you” or “an incident” - it’s something many women experience daily.

The NLP workshop was more theoretical than I expected, focusing on efficient way to represent probabilities. I'd love to get to the nitty-gritty of the implementation for parts of speech tagging, but it was still quite a useful setup for thinking about language processing.

Code retreat

For the code retreat we did pair-programmed, test-driven Game of Life in four runs. My coding partners were (I guess "J" was the letter of the day):

  • Jade for the initial Python look at the problem, with extremely detailed unit tests, which was a great starting point,
  • Juan for a little bit more broad approach to it in Ruby, which taught me a lot about best practices in a very short time,
  • James for the second try in Python, this time trying it with sub-matrices and using the "neighbourhood" of a cell as a starting point,
  • Juliano for some playtime with Elixir - we've had a great time pairing earlier this week, but today we were both way too tired to implement the problem or even recognise syntax problems.
The approach for the exercise was doing it test-first and throwing away the code after a pairing session. The rules for each one were:
  • ping-pong TDD - one person writes the test, the other one implementation and the next test, then the first one has to implement etc.,
  • 3 line limit on methods,
  • no primitive data types (focusing on expressing in problem domain terms),
  • no conditional statements/no speaking (the second one could've been used as an additional constraint for some languages, as it's sometimes natural to not use conditional statements).
I would love to write more about the code retreat, but I feel I need a few days to think about all the benefits of it and reevaluate my strategy before the next one (because, obviously, there has to be a next one).

Goal-setting workshop

The goal-setting workshop was a surprisingly successful short exercise. We worked in groups of three, working on one person's goals at a time. First they would present their focus for the RC, achievements to date and possible problems that are holding them back. I was pleasantly surprised how optimistic David and Sofia-Jeanne were about my goals and focus. The cute thing we did at the end of the workshop were paper cranes with our goals hidden inside, so we can unfold them at the end of our batch.

During the workshop we were also introduced to the concept of Test Driven Learning, which I think deserves a separate blog post as soon as I find some time.
