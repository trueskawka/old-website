RC - day 57

Achievements:

  • snapped out of an impostor syndrome episode
  • learned a lot about the codebase of Zulip
  • learned even more about the development process

It was very hard to start working on my Outreachy application on Tuesday. The process of learning how Zulip works, where my PR fits in the codebase and how am I to contribute something meaningful to a big project a lot of people use, was time-consuming and a bit intimidating. At some point I felt I’m not going to achieve much and should just stop pretending, giving a way in for other, more focused, talented and generally smarter people.

I’ve realised I have a severe case of the impostor syndrome and it was surprising for me. I got into RC - the programming Neverland, where everything is possible and failing is learning. Where people are self-motivated and curate a growth environment, that’s welcoming and non-intimidating. Even so, I’ve managed to convince myself subconsciously I don’t belong here. That I’ve wasted so much time over the years (especially other people’s time) and all my potential and learning is going to waste.

It’s much easier to talk and share things I feel confident about. My programming is going in the right direction, but it will take years for me to feel as confident as I am with my writing or business skills. And it might never even come to that, since the environment is constantly changing and the amount of learning required to stay on the top of my ability might be incomparable to most other settings.

It takes a village to grow. I am lucky enough to have friends that care about me and know when I need a nudge in the right direction. Cihan was there again to intimidate me into working and we’ve work together all night. I didn’t finish the pull request, but I’ve learned a lot on the way and started feeling confident again.

Now I think am at risk of being too much of a perfectionist, as ridiculous as it may sounds. I’m afraid of wasting someone’s time on a feature that’s not completely finished, so I’ll work on it more and try to push myself to share it by the end of the day on Wednesday.

  • understand deeply

    Don’t face complex issues head-on; first understand simple ideas deeply. Be brutally honest about what you know and don’t know. Then see what’s missing, identify the gaps, and fill them in. There are degrees to understanding and you can always heighten yours.

  • make mistakes

    Fail to succeed. Intentionally get it wrong to inevitably get it even more right. Mistakes highlight unforeseen opportunities and holes in your understanding.

  • raise questions

    Constantly create questions to clarify and extend your understanding.

  • follow ideas

    Look back to see where ideas came from and then look ahead to discover where those ideas may lead. Following the consequences of small ideas can result in big payoffs.

  • change

    You can always improve, grow, and extract more out of your education. Change is the universal constant that allows you to get the most out of learning.

ElixirConf 2016 - guest post by Juliano

Hi, if you’re reading this (especially Alicja’s Polish translation), then you certainly don’t know me yet. Let me introduce myself.

I’m Juliano, Alicja’s batch-mate from Brazil!

Now that that’s out of the way, let me tell you about Elixir and ElixirConf 2016.

Elixir is an up and coming programming language made by another Brazilian. It leverages a lot of ancient technology (Erlang) and is built in a modern, attractive and well thought out way, making awesome stuff accessible to a broader range of programmers. The community is pretty awesome. People are genuinely friendly and patient and they make an effort to make things accessible to everyone at every level.

ElixirConf 2016 was set in the Disney Swan and Dolphin Resert. It was raining hard and natives from Orlando were talking about “hurricane season”, which I thought was more exciting than Disney itself.

Ever since I arrived to NY I’ve been waiting for a flu to catch me. The weather was very hot, the subway stations were even hotter, and the trains were freezing cold. To me, the NY subway system is like a massive human pasteurization machine, where they successively bake and freeze you between train stops and connections, until all of your bacteria are dead. The cold arrived right on time for ElixirConf and I spent my first conference day with a fever, sore throat and an artificially deep voice. Even so, it was awesome and well worth it.

ElixirConf has doubled in size compared to last year. I was there too during ElixirConf 2015, and actually got a free copy of “Seven More Programming Languages in Seven Weeks” as a “Longest Distance Travelled” Award. We were some 200 programmers flying in from a lot of different places to meet other people excited about this cool new language with an uncertain future. Very few of us were actually using Elixir in our day jobs. Things have changed a bit. This year we were 542 and around 30% of attendees say they run Elixir in their day jobs!

We are a heavy flock of newbies coming into a technology stack that has been there since the 80’s, so there is eventually some friction. Some old-time Erlang developers are not happy with that, which became fuel for jokes in some presentations.

This year, many of the talks were from companies actively using Elixir, talking about their success stories and things they learned. I personally like that kind of presentations a lot.

Conferences are, in the end, much more than just the talks. I’ve met some very nice people from Brazil who were also attending and had a chance to talk at length with some of Elixir’s creators and core contributors. Meeting again with people I’ve seen last year gave the conference sort of a feeling of family, and knowing that many of them, like me, are still not using Elixir at work made me feel that Jessica Kerr’s tear-inducing keynote from last year (here) still echoed in everyone’s hearts.

I’ve also been high-fived by a Michigan fan and approached by two Detroit residents during the conference because I bought a Michigan hoodie at the Detroit airport so I wouldn’t freeze to death during my 10 hour connection.

The closing keynote was done by Boyd Multerer, former engineering leader for Microsoft’s Xbox console for the last 14 years. He gave a very thought provoking presentation and spoke at length about why he chose Elixir and Erlang as his career investment for the next 10 years. It was a very rewarding talk to watch, hearing Boyd come up with compelling reasons why, for him, the Erlang VM and Elixir are the best fit for technology in the next decade, giving confirmation to the intellectual, emotional and financial investment we all had in the language.

I left early, still feeling a little bit feverish, but very much inspired to go back to the RC and write more Elixir.

Code retreat

For the code retreat we did pair-programmed, test-driven Game of Life in four runs. My coding partners were (I guess "J" was the letter of the day):

  • Jade for the initial Python look at the problem, with extremely detailed unit tests, which was a great starting point,
  • Juan for a little bit more broad approach to it in Ruby, which taught me a lot about best practices in a very short time,
  • James for the second try in Python, this time trying it with sub-matrices and using the "neighbourhood" of a cell as a starting point,
  • Juliano for some playtime with Elixir - we've had a great time pairing earlier this week, but today we were both way too tired to implement the problem or even recognise syntax problems.
The approach for the exercise was doing it test-first and throwing away the code after a pairing session. The rules for each one were:
  • ping-pong TDD - one person writes the test, the other one implementation and the next test, then the first one has to implement etc.,
  • 3 line limit on methods,
  • no primitive data types (focusing on expressing in problem domain terms),
  • no conditional statements/no speaking (the second one could've been used as an additional constraint for some languages, as it's sometimes natural to not use conditional statements).
I would love to write more about the code retreat, but I feel I need a few days to think about all the benefits of it and reevaluate my strategy before the next one (because, obviously, there has to be a next one).

The goal-setting workshop was a surprisingly successful short exercise. We worked in groups of three, working on one person's goals at a time.
