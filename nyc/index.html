Curiouser and curiouser
  • If you swipe your Metrocard and don’t pass fast enough, you’ll have to wait 15 minutes to use it again at the same station. At least I get to walk a lot to the next station.
  • The subway always has some changes in routes, times and entrances. You’ll most probably learn about them after waiting 40+ minutes on the wrong station.
  • It must be really hard to be in a wheelchair here. I can’t imagine using public transport or most of the sidewalks.
  • If you never felt race is an issue (and being Polish makes it highly improbable), you will feel very <insert your skin colour here>. It is somehow very important in NYC (and maybe even in the US in general). I look white and American until I start speaking. They’re generally much nicer when they notice my accent.
  • Amazon probably won’t accept you European debit card if you want to use it with a Polish address. Or any address. They just don’t want my money.
  • Second-hand awkwardness is almost guaranteed, if you’re not used to people randomly singing/dancing/talking to you in public.

09/04/2016 19:46

Central Park trip

I finally went to Central Park. Starting at the very top at 110th St and exiting nearly two hours later around the 59th St, it was a calming and wonderful experience. During the walk I completely forgot I’m in a vast and restless city.

It reminded me partially of London parks, especially the enormous size of Regent’s Park, but it was far less orderly and thus more interesting. It looks like a preserved part of a once wild environment, which is strange considering how much planning went into creating it.

As I grew accustomed to in NYC, people were dancing, singing, playing and laughing loudly, there was hardly any quiet space by the time I got to the Alice in Wonderland Statue (I couldn’t get a photo without a horde of children climbing on it). Here you can get lost completely, but never away from people.

08/21/2016 14:35

first sightseeing trip

Went sightseeing with a group of RC friends. We decided to start early in the morning, as most tourists would probably sleep in.

The One World Observatory was both amazing and tacky. The elevator ride was scary but loads of fun and took about 47 seconds to go up 102 floors. The views were amazing - just seeing the city and knowing how tall the tiny buildings below are. But the commercial side was a bit surprising and very tacky. The sales pitches, the useless big screens with loads of videos, the gift shop full of awful knick-knocks - everything to convince us it was worth spending more than $30 for the ride up.

The WTC memorial is amazing. Quiet, simple and peaceful. Two fountains flowing slowly and steadily into nothingness.

Sadly, when we got to Wall Street, there were so many tourists grabbing the bull statue by the balls, I didn't get to take a picture this time.

08/16/2016 13:15

New York quirks

  • you can't escape the heat in the subway - the stations are hotter and more humid and one can wait for a train up to 30-40 minutes
  • subway trains are really cold and you can't hear the announcements, unless it's 11pm, when the drivers suddenly know how to use the intercom and shout
  • the city never sleeps, at least not before midnight - stores, even tiny ones, are open, people are sitting on the stairs talking or walking around (I can understand socialising late, when temperature drops to manageable 25 degrees)
  • a broken water pipe is the source of joy for the entire neighbourhood, children and adults alike run around in the water and nobody even thinks about calling someone to fix it
  • shopping is a bit confusing, since they use net prices and I'm never quite sure what products are taxed, thus how much am I supposed to pay
  • they have paypass, but don't know what it is and how to use it
  • street lights have a countdown, which was a bit stressful at first as I couldn't see the numbers and believed crossing should take about 5 seconds - that got solved with jaywalking

it's a NYC thing

That's what my host told me when I complained about a cockroach infestation at the apartment.

Yesterday it was two small insects, one in the kitchen sink and the other one in my closet.

Today morning it was three of them in the sink and one in the bathroom. I've had the pleasure of filming his movements to document the encounter.

Now I'm afraid of looking into a drawer, since I've just met the new inhabitant - a huge roach nearly the size of my thumb.

I've contacted Airbnb, just to make sure I'll be eligible for a full refund. I've asked the host to get rid of the infestation, since I don't believe 7 cockroaches in the span of 24 hours count as a "problem that occurs very rarely".

It will be a bummer to leave the AC behind.
