RC - day 41

Achievements:

  • got a lot of rest
  • reflected on my first six weeks at RC
  • discovered my patronus

Six weeks ago I came to NYC with a very vague idea of what I wanted to do specifically. There seemed to be so many things I wanted and ‘had’ to learn - algorithms, networking, CS, ‘proper’ programming (whatever that means), machine learning, better visualisations, APIs, audio processing etc. The list could go on and on, as for the first few days I was utterly distracted and lost.

Over the course of the first half of my batch, I’ve tried and resigned from many things. I didn’t work enough on my ‘big’ project, I felt that some methods of learning or topics I wanted to learn do not resonate with me. I’ve been going to workshops, sometimes a few a day, trying to learn as much as possible. At the same time, I was constantly amazed by the degree of focus of some of my fellow batchers, especially those who came here for ‘only’ six weeks.

The health scare last week was a wake-up call - I’ve been overworking myself, overdosing on the wonderful experience that is RC. Yes, it was great to get up every morning full of plans and with my to-do list bursting. To rush for my morning meditation and check-in, feeling like I’m moving forward. But I need to shift my focus and adjust my goals, otherwise I might lose my motivation altogether. After all, it’s all about never graduating.

As such, I have a few areas and goals to focus on, distilled from the vast universe of possibilities:

  • I still want to build my language-learning app, a project I’ve spread into tiny tasks that are now somewhere near the end of my to-do list under an avalanche of ideas

  • I love talking to people and I’ve been doing a lot of that, but I want to talk more about programming, which means more pairing - at least part of it should be focused on pursuing my projects

  • shortlist of bigger projects:
    • language-learning app (possible pairing on audio processing, APIs and web dev)
    • learning React while building an app (I have some ideas I would like to work on, especially with Ivo)
    • building my own Markdown editor, either web or for MacOS
  • career goals:
    • writing 10 professional blog posts by the end of my stay in the US
    • applying for the remote opportunity I’m excited about (but don’t want to write about it too much, as not to jinx it)
    • having at least 2 interview prep sessions a week
  • productive procrastination:
    • an ongoing list of shorter projects to learn specific programming skills
    • making small 3D printed prototypes for future DYIing

  • 5 hours a week for a non-programming learning project

  • spending Sundays and most of Saturdays on sightseeing/socializing

All in all, the first half of my batch was a perspective-changing experience. I’ll work on the next six weeks to be even better.

Other than reflections, plans and rest, I’ve had a fun few minutes with discovering my patronus on Pottermore. Apparently it’s a weasel, which doesn’t seem too far removed from a squirrel I’ve always imagined it to be.

RC - day 34

Achievements:

  • watching Objectified
  • awesome women’s night at RC

Cats, books and Stranger Things kept me entertained most of the day. The kitties are playful and curious, constantly nagging me for attention. Focusing was not an easy task.

We’ve also had a great women’s night at RC, thanks to Isla & Kaylyn (and RC staff, of course), discussing some of our experiences and developing strategies and actionable solutions to common problems.

I especially loved two simple strategies presented by Alicia:

  • inappropriate comment

    • ask the person to repeat their comment, to avoid ambiguity (e.g. what did you say? what do you mean by that? excuse me?)
    • label the comment/language (e.g. that’s ignorant/sexist/dismissive), not the person
    • get back to work (after all, you are paid to work and hopefully so is the other person)

  • unwelcome behaviour

    Instead of reacting on the spot, it’s best to talk in person, e.g. “I feel like you interrupt me, have you noticed?” - that’s a simple yes or no question and both answers are great!

    1. yes - “good! please don’t do that again”
    2. no - “no problem, how about I just let you know if I notice it again?” (opening for further improvement)

It is much easier to react and solve problems when we can name them. And we don’t feel crazy, because it’s not “just you” or “an incident” - it’s something many women experience daily.

We also came up with an idea for an app to register unwanted behaviours and easily visualise them, which I hope to mob code with my awesome RC friends over the next week.

I’m rarely in all women environments and it was freeing to experience that. Without men around we behave differently, focusing on communication rather than perceptions.

Everything is great now.

Wednesday I had a health scare - I fainted on the subway and ended up in a hospital. Nothing much happened, apart from lightly hitting my head on the subway car.

The experience was really stressful because of the insurance. Instead of keeping my cool and being happy someone takes care of me, I was constantly trying to calculate if my travel insurance is going to cover it. That’s a very US experience.

What surprised me is that you get the bill for emergency care about 4-6 weeks after the incident and you actually get three bills:

  • one from the ambulance company, because the ambulances are privately owned
  • one from the hospital
  • one from the doctor that checks you up at the hospital

Altogether an ambulance ride, an ECG & a blood pressure check at the hospital and a two minute chat with the doctor can cost anything between $2.000 and $8.000. Good my insurance is for more than $30.000.

With the Airbnb problems and lack of productivity over the weekend, I felt lost and extremely stressed. Luckily, my loved ones and the RC community were there for me. I decided to take it slow.

ElixirConf 2016 - guest post by Juliano

Hi, if you’re reading this (especially Alicja’s Polish translation), then you certainly don’t know me yet. Let me introduce myself.

I’m Juliano, Alicja’s batch-mate from Brazil!

Now that that’s out of the way, let me tell you about Elixir and ElixirConf 2016.

Elixir is an up and coming programming language made by another Brazilian. It leverages a lot of ancient technology (Erlang) and is built in a modern, attractive and well thought out way, making awesome stuff accessible to a broader range of programmers. The community is pretty awesome. People are genuinely friendly and patient and they make an effort to make things accessible to everyone at every level.

ElixirConf 2016 was set in the Disney Swan and Dolphin Resert. It was raining hard and natives from Orlando were talking about “hurricane season”, which I thought was more exciting than Disney itself.

Ever since I arrived to NY I’ve been waiting for a flu to catch me. The weather was very hot, the subway stations were even hotter, and the trains were freezing cold. To me, the NY subway system is like a massive human pasteurization machine, where they successively bake and freeze you between train stops and connections, until all of your bacteria are dead. The cold arrived right on time for ElixirConf and I spent my first conference day with a fever, sore throat and an artificially deep voice. Even so, it was awesome and well worth it.

ElixirConf has doubled in size compared to last year. I was there too during ElixirConf 2015, and actually got a free copy of “Seven More Programming Languages in Seven Weeks” as a “Longest Distance Travelled” Award. We were some 200 programmers flying in from a lot of different places to meet other people excited about this cool new language with an uncertain future. Very few of us were actually using Elixir in our day jobs. Things have changed a bit. This year we were 542 and around 30% of attendees say they run Elixir in their day jobs!

We are a heavy flock of newbies coming into a technology stack that has been there since the 80’s, so there is eventually some friction. Some old-time Erlang developers are not happy with that, which became fuel for jokes in some presentations.

This year, many of the talks were from companies actively using Elixir, talking about their success stories and things they learned. I personally like that kind of presentations a lot.

Conferences are, in the end, much more than just the talks. I’ve met some very nice people from Brazil who were also attending and had a chance to talk at length with some of Elixir’s creators and core contributors. Meeting again with people I’ve seen last year gave the conference sort of a feeling of family, and knowing that many of them, like me, are still not using Elixir at work made me feel that Jessica Kerr’s tear-inducing keynote from last year (here) still echoed in everyone’s hearts.

I’ve also been high-fived by a Michigan fan and approached by two Detroit residents during the conference because I bought a Michigan hoodie at the Detroit airport so I wouldn’t freeze to death during my 10 hour connection.

The closing keynote was done by Boyd Multerer, former engineering leader for Microsoft’s Xbox console for the last 14 years. He gave a very thought provoking presentation and spoke at length about why he chose Elixir and Erlang as his career investment for the next 10 years. It was a very rewarding talk to watch, hearing Boyd come up with compelling reasons why, for him, the Erlang VM and Elixir are the best fit for technology in the next decade, giving confirmation to the intellectual, emotional and financial investment we all had in the language.

I left early, still feeling a little bit feverish, but very much inspired to go back to the RC and write more Elixir.

Code retreat

For the code retreat we did pair-programmed, test-driven Game of Life in four runs. My coding partners were (I guess "J" was the letter of the day):

  • Jade for the initial Python look at the problem, with extremely detailed unit tests, which was a great starting point,
  • Juan for a little bit more broad approach to it in Ruby, which taught me a lot about best practices in a very short time,

The approach for the exercise was doing it test-first and throwing away the code after a pairing session. The rules for each one were:
  • ping-pong TDD - one person writes the test, the other one implementation and the next test, then the first one has to implement etc.,
  • 3 line limit on methods,
  • no primitive data types (focusing on expressing in problem domain terms),
  • no conditional statements/no speaking (the second one could've been used as an additional constraint for some languages, as it's sometimes natural to not use conditional statements).
I would love to write more about the code retreat, but I feel I need a few days to think about all the benefits of it and reevaluate my strategy before the next one (because, obviously, there has to be a next one).

New York quirks

  • you can't escape the heat in the subway - the stations are hotter and more humid and one can wait for a train up to 30-40 minutes
  • subway trains are really cold and you can't hear the announcements, unless it's 11pm, when the drivers suddenly know how to use the intercom and shout
  • the city never sleeps, at least not before midnight - stores, even tiny ones, are open, people are sitting on the stairs talking or walking around (I can understand socialising late, when temperature drops to manageable 25 degrees)
  • a broken water pipe is the source of joy for the entire neighbourhood, children and adults alike run around in the water and nobody even thinks about calling someone to fix it
  • shopping is a bit confusing, since they use net prices and I'm never quite sure what products are taxed, thus how much am I supposed to pay
  • they have paypass, but don't know what it is and how to use it
  • street lights have a countdown, which was a bit stressful at first as I couldn't see the numbers and believed crossing should take about 5 seconds - that got solved with jaywalking
